Timber management plan Texas

Updated June 2026 · By Bob Rowe, Rowe Land, Timber & Dozer Services

A timber management plan is a written document that schedules thinnings, sets final-harvest timing, plans replant, lays out access roads and fire breaks, and aligns your stand with your goals — income, recreation, wildlife, legacy, or sale. Most plans cover a 30-to-40-year horizon and are updated every 5 years.

Owning timberland without a plan is like owning rental property without leases. The asset still works, but you're leaving money on the table and giving up control.

What a plan covers

  • Stand inventory — current age, species, density, volume
  • Thinning schedule — when to thin, how heavy, target residual density
  • Final harvest year — based on stand growth curves and goals
  • Replant plan — species selection, density, site prep, herbicide
  • Roads & access — how to get equipment in and logs out
  • Fire breaks — bordering ag fields, neighbors, structures
  • BMPs — Best Management Practices for water quality
  • Wildlife enhancements — food plots, edges, mast trees
  • Tax considerations — to discuss with your CPA

Who needs one

  • You own 20+ acres of pine or mixed stand
  • You inherited timberland and don't know what's on it
  • You're managing for long-term family ownership, not just one sale
  • You're absentee — don't live on the property
  • You want to keep ag valuation while running a real timber operation

What a plan does NOT do

It doesn't bind you. It's your plan, on paper, that you can change. It also doesn't replace the cruise that precedes any specific sale — a management plan is the long horizon; a cruise is the snapshot at sale time.

Frequently asked questions

Does Texas require a management plan?
Not generally, but a written plan can support ag/wildlife valuation and is required for certain cost-share programs (like NRCS EQIP).
How much does a plan cost?
Standalone plans typically run $400–$1,500 depending on tract size and detail. Bundled with our consulting work, we often include a basic plan.
How often does it get updated?
Every 5 years is standard. After major events (storm damage, beetle outbreak) — sooner.
Will a plan help with property taxes?
Often yes. A written plan supports the ag valuation and can document the “degree of intensity” counties look for. Talk to your appraisal district.
