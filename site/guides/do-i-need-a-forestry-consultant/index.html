Do I need a forestry consultant to sell timber?

Updated June 2026 · By Bob Rowe, Rowe Land, Timber & Dozer Services

If your timber sale is worth more than about $5,000, hiring a consultant typically returns more than it costs. Competitive bidding from a consultant-run sale routinely lifts the final price 15–40% — easily covering the 8–12% consulting fee. The Texas A&M Forest Service formally recommends consultants for significant sales.

The honest answer is “most of the time, yes.” The exceptions are small enough that they're easy to identify.

When a consultant clearly pays for themselves

  • Stand is 10+ acres
  • Estimated sale value over $5,000
  • You've never done a timber sale before
  • You inherited the land and don't know what's on it
  • Someone offered you a price out of the blue
  • You're clearing for a build
  • You can't be on the property during the harvest

When a direct sale to a buyer is fine

  • Very small — a few trees for a personal sawmill
  • You already know the buyer well and have history together
  • Pure salvage where one nearby mill is the only realistic buyer

Frequently asked questions

How is a consultant paid?
Typically as a percentage of final sale price (8–12%), paid from the proceeds. So the fee is essentially self-funded by the higher price competitive bidding produces.
Can I get bids without a consultant?
Theoretically yes — but qualified buyers will rarely take a homemade RFP seriously. A consultant brings the relationships, the cruise data, and the contract template.
What if my timber is worth less than $5,000?
A consultant may still make sense if you don't know what you have. For very small sales, talk to us first — we'll tell you straight whether it's worth hiring out.
Are consultants licensed in Texas?
Not by the state. That's why credentials, references, and a clear written agreement matter.
